About Muhammad Ali

Muhammad Ali (born Cassius Marcellus Clay Jr.; January 17, 1942 â€“ June 3, 2016) was an American professional boxer and cultural icon. He is widely considered to be one of the greatest boxers of all time and is known for his lightning-fast footwork, quick jab, and unparalleled charisma both in and out of the ring. Ali first gained national attention at the age of 18 when he won a gold medal in the light heavyweight division at the 1960 Summer Olympics in Rome. He then turned professional and quickly rose to fame with his unique style of boxing, which emphasized his speed, agility, and strategic footwork. Throughout his career, Ali was involved in some of the most iconic fights in boxing history, including his three epic bouts against Joe Frazier and his famous "Rumble in the Jungle" match against George Foreman in 1974. In addition to his impressive boxing career, Ali was also known for his social and political activism. He was a vocal advocate for civil rights and spoke out against the Vietnam War, which led to his suspension from boxing for several years. Despite his battles both inside and outside the ring, Ali remained a beloved figure throughout his life and continues to be an inspiration to people all over the world.
